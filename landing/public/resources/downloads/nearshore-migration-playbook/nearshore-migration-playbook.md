# 1. Executive summary

**Who this is for:** Heads of Procurement and Supply Chain at companies with €50M-€1B in annual spend, currently sourcing 40%+ from a single country (typically China), with board-level mandate to diversify.

**Three things you'll get from this playbook:**

1. **A country shortlist that matches your categories.** Not every nearshore country fits every category. Poland for metals is not Portugal for textiles. The matrix on page 5 and the category pages (8-10) give you a defensible shortlist, not a generic ranking.

2. **A 90-day pilot plan.** Most nearshoring programs fail because they try to move 5 categories at once and overwhelm qualification capacity. The staged plan on page 12 moves one pilot category in 90 days with a clear decision point before the second category starts.

3. **A business case that survives CFO review.** The model on page 13 shows the total cost of diversification (tooling, audit, higher unit cost, dual-source inventory) against the expected-value cost of a single-country disruption. Worked example with the numbers the CFO asks for.

**What this playbook does not do:**

- It does not recommend one country over all others universally. Country choice depends on category, volume, lead-time sensitivity, and regulatory context.
- It does not cover US reshoring (different labour economics, IRA/CHIPS subsidy dynamics).
- It does not replace on-the-ground due diligence. The playbook tells you where to look; you still need to visit, audit, qualify.

**The core argument:**

Diversification costs money in year 1 and saves money in year 2-5 under most scenarios — but it guarantees optionality against the tail-risk scenarios that single-country strategies do not survive. The Ukraine war, Covid, the Red Sea, and the 2025 tariff round all cost specific companies 3-12% of revenue. Companies with nearshore options absorbed the shock; single-country companies passed the cost to customers, who then looked elsewhere.

Diversification is insurance. The question is not whether to buy it, but which policy and how much.

# 2. The 2026 China+1 context

Five shifts since 2020 changed the math.

**Shift 1: Freight volatility is structural, not cyclical.** Red Sea routing since 2023 adds 10-14 days and 15-25% freight cost for Asia-Europe trade, and there is no reversion in sight. Factor an 8-12% shipping surcharge above 2019 baselines for any Asia-origin calculation.

**Shift 2: Tariffs are a permanent variable, not a one-time event.** US Section 301 expanded in 2024 and again in 2025. The EU added anti-subsidy duties on Chinese EVs and photovoltaics. Even buyers who don't import to tariff-affected jurisdictions see spillover through their customers.

**Shift 3: CBAM is live.** As of 2026, the EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism requires verified emissions data on iron, steel, aluminium, cement, fertilisers, electricity, and hydrogen imports from non-EU countries. For Chinese steel, the estimated CBAM cost adds €30-50 per tonne in 2026, escalating to €80-150 per tonne when full implementation phases complete 2027-2028.

**Shift 4: Nearshore cost gaps are narrowing.** Chinese labour cost inflation ran 8-12% annually 2020-2024. Turkish lira depreciation made Turkish labour cheaper in EUR terms. Polish, Romanian, Hungarian labour is still 40-60% of German and 2-3× of Turkish, but the gap to China has compressed materially.

**Shift 5: Multi-language supplier discovery is newly feasible.** This is the part that often gets missed. The reason companies stayed single-country isn't because diversification was unwanted; it's because finding and qualifying nearshore suppliers was 30+ hours per category, and that cost scaled linearly with the number of categories you wanted to move. AI-native supplier discovery (disclosure: Procurea is one such tool — so are others) compressed that first qualification stage from 30 hours to under 1 hour. Which changed the calculus.

When the tools cost 10x less and the geopolitical costs of doing nothing went up 10x, the decision tipped for a lot of people. That's where 2026 is different from 2019.

# 4. Country deep-dive: Poland, Turkey, Portugal

## Poland

**Best for:** Machined metals, metal stamping, plastic injection, automotive tier suppliers, electro-mechanical assembly.

**Strengths:** Dense mid-market manufacturing base (many supplier choices in the 50-500 employee band — the sweet spot for custom-tooled parts). Strong ISO 9001 / IATF 16949 adoption. English-speaking commercial teams in cities like Wroclaw, Poznan, Gdansk, Rzeszow. 2-4 day road delivery to Germany/France. Family-owned businesses often mean responsive ownership.

**Weaknesses:** Labour cost inflation averaged 7-10% annually 2022-2025 as Polish wages converged with German. At current trends, Poland's labour cost advantage over Germany shrinks by 2-3 percentage points per year — so today's 25% gap is 10-15% gap by 2030.

**Typical category fit:** Automotive machined parts, medical device housings, construction hardware, furniture components, appliance assemblies.

**Watch-outs:** Capacity can be tight — expansion of skilled operators is the bottleneck, not capital. Suppliers with high utilisation (>75%) deprioritise smaller new customers.

## Turkey

**Best for:** Textiles, apparel, leather goods, metals (aluminum, steel, copper), appliances, glass, ceramics.

**Strengths:** The largest textile manufacturing ecosystem outside Asia. Customs Union with EU = no tariff on most goods to EU. EUR-denominated quotes increasingly common (suppliers carry their own FX risk). Strong port infrastructure (Istanbul, Izmir). Short lead time to Eastern Europe (4-5 days road to Poland, 2-3 days to Bulgaria/Romania).

**Weaknesses:** Lira volatility means suppliers may renegotiate mid-contract when the currency moves sharply — not formally, but through "adjusted material costs" or similar. Non-EU status means CBAM applies for covered categories. Political risk premium (mid).

**Typical category fit:** Fashion textiles, technical textiles, home textiles, aluminum extrusions, steel service centres, glassware, white goods assemblies.

**Watch-outs:** Size tier matters — Turkey has world-class multinationals and small family workshops with dramatically different reliability profiles. Qualify specifically on who will do the work, not the organisation name.

## Portugal

**Best for:** Footwear, leather goods, textiles (high-end), growing electronics, furniture, cork.

**Strengths:** Atlantic port access (Leixoes, Sines) for both EU and Americas-bound goods. EU member + EUR. Strong design/engineering capability for fashion categories. English and French commercial fluency. Quality culture that approaches German levels in leather and high-end textiles.

**Weaknesses:** Workforce demographics — Portugal has aging population + emigration of younger skilled workers. Capacity growth is constrained. Higher labour cost than Romania, Bulgaria, Hungary for comparable categories.

**Typical category fit:** Premium leather goods, high-end fashion manufacturing, orthopaedic footwear, cork products, furniture (especially upholstered), growing low-volume electronics assembly.

**Watch-outs:** Capacity constraints mean new customers often wait in queue behind established relationships. Start relationship-building early; don't expect immediate supply.

# 5. Country deep-dive: Romania, Hungary, Czechia

## Romania

**Best for:** Automotive wire harnesses, plastic injection, mid-complexity assembly, garments (lower-cost end), metal fabrication.

**Strengths:** Lowest labour cost in EU (still €7-9/hr manufacturing), EUR-adjacent via leu stability vs EUR. Strong Dacia / Ford automotive cluster creates tier-2 supplier density. Growing electronics assembly. EU member, no CBAM, no tariff.

**Weaknesses:** Skilled worker availability tightening as multinationals (Bosch, Continental, Dacia, Ford) consume capacity. English proficiency in commercial teams more variable than Poland — expect French, German, or English depending on the region (Transylvania more German-influenced, Bucharest more English).

**Typical category fit:** Automotive electrical (harnesses, connectors), garment sewing (lower-cost end of textile chain), plastic-injected auto/appliance parts, metal fabrication for construction.

**Watch-outs:** Quality variance wider than in Poland — qualification rigour matters more. Do facility visits; don't rely on remote audits for strategic categories.

## Hungary

**Best for:** Automotive OEM-tier supply (Audi, BMW, Mercedes, Suzuki clusters), electronics assembly, precision machining.

**Strengths:** Automotive OEM density creates mature tier-1 and tier-2 supplier base with IATF 16949 certification as table stakes. German commercial language strong (historical and ongoing OEM relationships). Fast road link to Germany (18-20 hours from Budapest).

**Weaknesses:** Labour cost has climbed — now close to Polish/Czech levels with less of the mid-market density. Politics-business interface less predictable than some peers (for purchasing this usually doesn't bite, but worth flagging).

**Typical category fit:** Automotive tier-2 stampings and moulded parts, precision machined subassemblies, electronics contract manufacturing.

**Watch-outs:** Suppliers often have single large OEM customer relationships that take priority — confirm your order won't sit in a queue behind OEM releases.

## Czechia

**Best for:** Precision machining, tooling, high-complexity assembly, industrial machinery, electro-mechanical components.

**Strengths:** Strong engineering education pipeline (Prague, Brno technical universities). High automation adoption — Czech suppliers often compete with Germany on precision at 30-40% lower cost. Short lead time to Germany (1-3 days road). Strong Škoda / VW cluster creates automotive excellence. High ISO 9001 and IATF 16949 adoption.

**Weaknesses:** Highest labour cost among the six (€11-14/hr). Tight capacity — the "good" suppliers are often booked 3-6 months ahead for new business. English proficiency varies; German often stronger.

**Typical category fit:** Precision machined parts, custom tooling, industrial machinery components, electronic assemblies requiring higher complexity, optical components.

**Watch-outs:** Price discipline is weaker than Poland or Turkey — Czech suppliers know their precision niche and price accordingly. Negotiation on unit price is harder; negotiation on terms and service is easier.

# 6. Textiles and apparel

**Primary recommendation: Turkey**

**Secondary: Portugal (premium), Poland (technical textiles)**

**Not recommended: Romania (limited capability above basic sewing), Czechia / Hungary (no textile base)**

Turkey's textile industry is structurally different from every alternative: vertically integrated from yarn spinning through weaving / knitting through dyeing, finishing, and garment making, often within a 100km radius of Istanbul or Denizli. This is why a t-shirt produced in Turkey can lead-time at 21 days; the equivalent in Portugal takes 35-45 days because weaving might be sourced from Italy or Turkey anyway.

**What Turkey does exceptionally well:**
- Denim (Burç, Bossa, ISKO — world scale)
- Home textiles (Yataş, İpekyol)
- Technical textiles (Mocan, Kipaş)
- Fashion CMT (cut-make-trim) with fast turn

**What to source from Portugal instead:**
- Leather goods and footwear (Cresta, Carvalhos, Kyaia — European luxury tier)
- High-end fashion with design integration
- Cork-based textiles (niche but growing)
- Small-batch production runs for direct-to-consumer brands

**What to source from Poland:**
- Technical textiles (automotive interior, medical, industrial)
- Workwear (Gatta, Arlen)
- Home textiles for EU domestic market when lead time <14 days is critical

**Costs (2026):**
- Turkey basic sewing: €0.90-1.80 per minute CMT
- Portugal premium: €1.60-2.80 per minute CMT
- Poland technical: €1.40-2.20 per minute CMT
- China equivalent: €0.50-1.10 per minute CMT (but add 30-45 days freight + tariff/CBAM)

**Cautions:**
- Social compliance matters intensely in textiles — SMETA or BSCI audit within 18 months is non-negotiable for a Western buyer with ESG exposure.
- Certification of origin disputes (transshipment via Malaysia or Vietnam for Chinese fabric labelled "Made in Portugal") do happen. Verify origin on the actual fabric, not just the garment label.

# 7. Electronics and electromechanical

**Primary recommendation: Hungary or Czechia (complex), Poland (mid-complexity)**

**Secondary: Portugal (growing), Romania (growing automotive electronics)**

**Not primary: Turkey (strong in appliances, not SMT-dense)**

European electronics manufacturing services (EMS) is concentrated in Hungary, Czechia, and Poland — driven by the automotive OEM cluster. A PCBA (printed circuit board assembly) operation in Hungary often has the same quality and scale as a similar operation in mainland China; the price premium is 15-30%, offset by 35 days of freight savings.

**Category splits:**

- **Consumer electronics PCBA**: Hungary, Czechia, Poland for mid-to-high complexity. Expect €40-90/hour SMT line rate vs China's €25-40.
- **Automotive electronics**: Hungary has the OEM cluster (Audi, Mercedes, BMW, Suzuki all have Hungarian manufacturing). Suppliers are IATF 16949 and have PPAP fluency.
- **Power electronics / drives**: Czechia and Poland have strong capability, often as subsidiaries of German parent companies.
- **Appliance electronics (home white goods)**: Turkey via Arçelik / Vestel supply chains has a large base of electronics assembly for white goods, but less useful for custom products.

**Costs (2026):**
- Hungary SMT line with operator: €55-70/hr
- Poland SMT line with operator: €50-65/hr
- Czechia SMT line with operator: €60-80/hr
- Portugal SMT (growing base): €45-60/hr
- Romania (limited): €40-55/hr
- China reference: €25-40/hr + 35 days + 2% customs

**Lead time advantage:** 2-4 day road delivery to Western Europe customers vs 35-42 days sea from China. For working-capital-sensitive categories (consumer electronics with 3-6 month SKU lifecycle), this shortens your inventory cycle by 30-40%.

**Cautions:**
- Component sourcing is still global — even a Hungarian-assembled PCB has components from Asia. This means you've not eliminated geopolitical exposure; you've split it across assembly and component tiers. Worth modelling.
- Tooling amortization on custom PCBs is 6-10 weeks minimum in Europe; in China 3-5 weeks is normal. Factor into pilot timeline.

# 8. Metals, machined parts, and castings

**Primary recommendation: Poland (breadth), Czechia (precision), Turkey (cost + aluminium)**

**Secondary: Romania (lower-complexity), Hungary (automotive-tier)**

Metal fabrication is where CEE shines. Poland's machining workshops in Rzeszów (aviation cluster), Upper Silesia (heavy industry), Łódź (machine tools) have 40-year continuous industrial history and scale from single-lathe artisan to 500-machine corporate operations.

**Category-to-country mapping:**

- **Steel stampings / pressings**: Poland primary; Turkey for aluminum and larger stampings
- **CNC machining (mid-complexity)**: Poland, Czechia, Romania all viable; pick on capacity availability
- **Precision machining (high-complexity, 5-axis)**: Czechia lead, Poland close second
- **Casting (iron, steel)**: Poland (deep base), Czechia for precision castings
- **Aluminum extrusions**: Turkey (world-scale, EUR-competitive due to lira), Poland for smaller runs
- **Sheet metal fabrication**: Poland, Czechia, Hungary all strong

**Costs (2026, illustrative machined steel part, 500 units/year):**
- Poland: €8-14/unit (typical)
- Czechia: €10-16/unit (higher precision capability)
- Turkey: €6-10/unit (steel or aluminum specifically)
- Romania: €7-12/unit (simpler parts)
- Hungary: €9-14/unit (automotive-tier premium)
- China: €4-8/unit, but CBAM adds €0.30-0.80, freight adds €0.50-1.50, tooling amortization same

**When China's unit price advantage vanishes on metals:**
1. Low volume (<1000 units) — tooling amortization dominates
2. Tight tolerance (below ±0.05mm) — precision premium narrows gap
3. Complex tool (multi-cavity, multi-action) — Chinese tool cost is close to European
4. Regulated sector (medical, aerospace) — certification work neutralises China advantage

**Cautions:**
- CBAM applies to steel and aluminium from non-EU origins — Turkey included. For iron/steel specifically, Turkey's CBAM exposure in 2026 is modest (€0.08-0.18/kg typical) but compounds with freight costs.
- Raw material hedging: European suppliers often pass-through raw material index movements, Turkish suppliers sometimes absorb briefly. Know your supplier's stance.

# 9. Plastics, packaging, and converted materials

**Primary recommendation: Poland, Czechia, Turkey (each strong)**

**Secondary: Hungary (for automotive-integrated suppliers), Romania (lower-cost)**

Plastic injection molding and blow molding have strong CEE and Turkish bases. Mold tooling is the critical path: a Polish or Czech toolmaker takes 10-14 weeks for a production-ready mold; a Chinese toolmaker takes 6-10 weeks. The sea freight on the mold itself (4-6 weeks) usually closes the gap.

**Category splits:**

- **Injection molding (technical parts)**: Poland, Czechia, Hungary
- **Injection molding (packaging caps, closures)**: Poland, Turkey
- **Blow molding (bottles, containers)**: Poland, Turkey for basic; Czechia for premium
- **Corrugated packaging**: Poland (large base), Romania (growing)
- **Flexible packaging (films, laminates)**: Poland, Turkey
- **Labels and printing**: Poland strong (EU market leader in private-label printing)

**Cost indicators (injection molded part, 50g, 100k units/year):**
- Poland: €0.22-0.38/unit
- Czechia: €0.26-0.42/unit
- Turkey: €0.18-0.32/unit (note TRY volatility)
- Romania: €0.20-0.32/unit
- China: €0.14-0.24/unit + 5-8% freight + 10-15% tooling amortization on first-year volume

**Lead time advantages:** 3-5 days road delivery Poland→Germany vs 35-42 days sea from China. For seasonal packaging (holiday promotions, new product launches), the lead time difference often dominates cost.

**Cautions:**
- Recyclability and PPWR (EU Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation, in force 2026) increasingly drive material choices. EU suppliers are ahead of Chinese on PPWR compliance.
- Food-contact certifications (EU 10/2011) are non-negotiable for food packaging — verify with the test certificates, not self-declaration.

# 10. Sequencing strategy: pilot → parallel → ramp

Three things kill nearshoring programs. Sequencing fixes all three.

**Killer #1: Starting too big.** A program that tries to move 5 categories simultaneously runs out of qualification bandwidth, capable buyers, and executive attention. By month 4, two categories are half-done, two haven't started, one is being rolled back. The learning cost is wasted because the failures are all attributed to "nearshoring is hard" rather than to "moving 5 things at once is hard."

**Killer #2: Not running parallel long enough.** A program that cuts over from China to Poland in a single quarter before the Polish supplier has proven 6 months of consistent supply is taking a reliability risk that the business case didn't account for.

**Killer #3: No decision point.** A program that never defines "how do we know it's working?" drifts. At month 9 someone asks about ROI. At month 15 the board asks for a status. Without pre-declared success criteria, the program's fate depends on who's asking.

The fix is a staged sequence: **pilot → parallel → ramp**, with decision points between stages.

## Stage 1: Pilot (Months 0-4)

- **Scope:** 1 category, 15-25% of that category's volume, moved to 1-2 nearshore suppliers.
- **Purpose:** Prove the operational mechanics. Tooling transfer, quality ramp, first 3 deliveries on time.
- **Decision point at month 4:** If 3 consecutive deliveries meet quality and on-time targets → proceed to Parallel. If not → debug and extend pilot or kill (and learn).

## Stage 2: Parallel (Months 4-10)

- **Scope:** Same 1 category, scale to 40-60% of volume on nearshore, while maintaining China supply for resilience. Add a second category in parallel starting around month 6.
- **Purpose:** Test volume scalability, seasonality, exception handling. Confirm the business case with 6 months of real cost data (not forecasted).
- **Decision point at month 10:** Business case confirmed on the original category → proceed to Ramp. Second category performance tested → decide if it's ready for Parallel stage.

## Stage 3: Ramp (Months 10-24)

- **Scope:** Scale to target allocation (often 70/30 or 80/20 nearshore/China). Add additional categories one or two at a time based on qualification bandwidth.
- **Purpose:** Institutionalise the new supply base. Supplier development, second-source within nearshore, long-term agreements.
- **Decision point at month 24:** Full retrospective on the multi-year program. Document wins and losses. Recalibrate country choices based on actual performance vs. initial assumptions.

**Critical:** Do not skip stages. Moving from pilot straight to ramp (common in executive-led programs with aggressive timelines) almost always produces a disruption you're not equipped to handle.

# 11. 90-day pilot plan (week by week)

This is the executable plan for Stage 1 (Pilot) of the category you selected first.

## Week 1-2: Prepare

- [ ] **Category selection finalised.** Document why this category, not another (criteria: moderate complexity, sufficient volume to matter, at least 3 viable nearshore sources, lowest switching cost).
- [ ] **Success criteria defined.** What "the pilot worked" means quantitatively: on-time rate target, quality PPM target, landed cost delta acceptable band.
- [ ] **Kickoff meeting.** Category manager, supply chain, quality, commercial, finance. 60 minutes. One decision: we are doing this, here's who does what.
- [ ] **Baseline documented.** Current supplier, current unit cost, current lead time, current PPM. You cannot measure success without this.

## Week 3-4: Source

- [ ] **Supplier discovery.** Find 20-40 candidate nearshore suppliers. Traditionally 30 hours of manual work across LinkedIn, trade fairs, Google, industry directories. AI-native tools (Procurea and others) compress this to ~1 hour; either way, you need a long list before a short list.
- [ ] **Initial screening.** Reduce 20-40 down to 8-12 viable candidates based on capability, capacity, certifications, country fit.
- [ ] **RFQ prepared and sent.** Structured RFQ (use the RFQ Comparison Template). 10-day response window.

## Week 5-6: Qualify

- [ ] **Quotes received and scored.** Using the Vendor Scoring Framework. Shortlist: 2-3 suppliers.
- [ ] **Virtual or on-site audits.** For strategic categories, always on-site. For commodity, a detailed virtual (factory tour via video + certificate review + reference check) can suffice.
- [ ] **Supplier risk checklist run.** The 20-point checklist. Red flags addressed or candidate dropped.
- [ ] **Final supplier selected.** Document the decision rationale (audit trail template from Vendor Scoring Framework).

## Week 7-8: Tool and prepare

- [ ] **Tooling transferred or fabricated.** Lead time for custom tooling 6-12 weeks depending on complexity. If tooling exists at the Chinese supplier, transfer logistics (shipping, customs, installation at new supplier, first-article inspection) take 3-6 weeks.
- [ ] **Quality plan aligned.** Incoming inspection protocol, SPC limits, first-article requirements.
- [ ] **Commercial documents signed.** Supply agreement, NDA, quality agreement, logistics agreement.
- [ ] **Logistics set up.** Freight forwarder, customs broker (if applicable), warehouse receiving alignment.

## Week 9-10: First article + PPAP

- [ ] **First article produced.** 10-50 units depending on category.
- [ ] **Inspection and qualification.** Your quality team signs off. If medical / automotive / aerospace, formal PPAP submission.
- [ ] **Go/no-go decision.** Document.

## Week 11-13: First production run

- [ ] **PO issued for 15-25% of category volume.**
- [ ] **First production batch delivered.** Measure: on-time, quality, communication quality, exception handling.
- [ ] **30-day in-production review.** Data-driven check. Tee up the month-4 stage-gate decision.

**Outputs at end of 90 days:**
1. One new nearshore supplier in production, delivering 15-25% of category volume.
2. Documented performance data (quality, OTD, landed cost, vs. baseline).
3. Proven internal process (who does what, what's the rhythm).
4. Prepared to enter Parallel stage.

"We propose to diversify [Category X] from 100% China to 60% nearshore (Poland or Turkey) over 18 months. Expected year-1 cost impact: +[€Y]. Expected year 2+ annual cost: +/-[€Z]. # 13. ROI Model with worked example

## Model structure

**Year 1 costs (diversification investment):**

| Line | Typical range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Tooling transfer or duplication | €10k-80k per tool | Depends on complexity |
| Supplier qualification (internal) | €15k-40k per supplier | 40-120 hours × loaded rate + travel |
| Virtual + on-site audits | €3k-10k per supplier | Third-party if no internal team |
| First-article inspection | €2k-6k per part family | Lab fees + internal time |
| Unit cost differential (pilot volume) | (Nearshore price - China price) × pilot units | Can be negative if TCO favours nearshore |
| Safety stock during parallel stage | 60-90 days incremental stock | Working capital cost |
| Commercial / legal setup | €3k-8k | Contract negotiation, translation |

**Years 2-5 benefits:**

- Lead time reduction → inventory days reduced → working capital freed
- Freight cost reduction
- CBAM avoidance (covered categories)
- Tariff avoidance
- FX volatility hedged (EUR-zone sourcing for EUR-reporting buyer)
- Lower "risk-adjusted" unit cost once disruption-scenario probability factored

**Years 2-5 ongoing costs:**

- Unit price premium (nearshore > China in many categories)
- Dual-supplier management overhead (buyer time)
- Ongoing qualification maintenance

## Worked example: machined metal component

**Category:** Machined steel component, 120,000 units/year, currently 100% from Shenzhen supplier at €4.80/unit landed cost (EUR).

**Proposal:** Diversify 60% to Polish supplier at €6.20/unit landed cost over 18 months.

**Year 1 diversification cost:**

| Line | Amount |
|---|---|
| Tooling duplication (2 tools) | €35,000 |
| Qualification (internal 100h + travel) | €18,000 |
| On-site audit (third party) | €4,500 |
| First-article inspection | €3,000 |
| Unit differential on pilot 20k units × €1.40 | €28,000 |
| Safety stock (60 days × 20k units × €4.80 × 20% WACC) | €6,400 (working capital cost) |
| Commercial / legal | €5,000 |
| **Year 1 total incremental cost** | **€99,900** |

**Year 2 steady state (60% Poland / 40% China):**

- Poland volume: 72,000 × €6.20 = €446,400
- China volume: 48,000 × €4.80 = €230,400
- Total year 2: €676,800
- Year 2 if remained 100% China: 120,000 × €4.80 = €576,000
- **Annual premium: €100,800 (+17.5%)**

**Years 2-5 benefits (annualised):**

- Lead time reduction (56d → 21d average on Polish portion): inventory reduction of ~35 days × 72k units × €4.80 × 20% WACC = **€4,800 annual saving**
- Freight saving Poland vs China (€0.45 freight/unit × 72k units): **€32,400 annual saving**
- CBAM avoidance on Polish portion (€0.42 × 72k units): **€30,240 annual saving** (conservative — escalates 2027+)
- Risk-adjusted disruption saving: probability-weighted expected value of Red Sea / tariff / China-specific disruption × impact. Conservative estimate: 15% probability × 6 weeks disruption × €50k/week cost × 40% China residual exposure = **€18,000/year expected value**

**Annual Year 2+ benefit: ~€85,000-€120,000 (range on disruption scenario)**

**Net Year 2+ cost/(benefit): €100,800 - €100,000 = ~€800 annual cost** (essentially break-even before disruption scenarios)

**Under disruption scenarios (not if, when):**
- Red Sea persisting year-2: China portion lead time remains 56+ days vs baseline 42, incremental working capital ~€15k/year
- New tariff round: 10% tariff on China portion = €23,000/year, entirely avoided on Poland portion
- CBAM full implementation 2028: costs escalate proportionally

**Conclusion:** In the base case, the diversification is approximately cost-neutral year-on-year after year 1 recovery. Under any of three disruption scenarios rated >15% probability by 2026 consensus, diversification saves €30k-80k annually. The investment case is insurance.

# 15. Real patterns from Procurea beta cohort — anonymized composites

Three patterns we've observed in the beta cohort of Procurea users who ran diversification programs in 2025-2026. Composites, not individual companies.

## Pattern 1: The automotive tier-2 who moved stampings China → Poland+Turkey

A German automotive tier-2 stamping supplier with €85M revenue had 70% of their material (mostly uncoated steel, some aluminium) from Chinese and Vietnamese sources. After the Red Sea disruption added 12 days and €180k to quarterly freight costs, the board approved diversification.

Over 14 months they moved:
- Mild steel stampings: to Polish supplier (€4.50 → €5.10/unit, +13%; offset by 35-day lead time reduction and €28k/qtr freight saving)
- Aluminium components: to Turkish supplier (€2.80 → €2.95/unit, +5%; offset by EUR-denominated contract and avoided CBAM)
- Exotic alloys: retained Chinese supplier but reduced to 30% of category volume as hedge

**Landed cost delta year 1: +€220k vs baseline**. **Year 2: -€40k net** (freight saving + CBAM avoidance + working capital).

What went well: sequencing. They moved one category at a time over 14 months. No disruption to customer delivery despite running parallel supplies across three countries.

What almost went wrong: their Polish supplier had a 3-month quality issue on one stamping family (coating adhesion) that needed joint engineering intervention. The fact that they had kept 30% Chinese residual meant they could absorb the temporary gap without line-down events.

## Pattern 2: The consumer brand that moved textile production Turkey + Portugal

A mid-market consumer apparel brand (€40M revenue, 120 SKUs) had sourced 100% from Chinese CMT (cut-make-trim) partners. Lead time pressure (60 days + shipping = 75 days total from PO to warehouse) was constraining their fashion turn rate.

Over 12 months they moved:
- Commodity basics: Turkish CMT (€0.90/min vs China €0.60/min; +50% CMT cost but 21-day total lead time vs 75-day; allowed them to reduce overstock markdowns by ~€300k/year)
- Premium / design-led pieces: Portuguese CMT (€2.20/min, 30-day lead time; pricing power allowed them to absorb)
- Technical fabrics: retained Chinese fabric mill but CMT in Turkey

**Year 1 cost impact: +€180k CMT premium**
**Year 1 offset: -€300k reduced overstock markdown; -€80k air freight (eliminated)**
**Net year 1: €200k improvement**

What went well: the move enabled a faster design-to-shelf cycle, which was a commercial advantage they hadn't quantified at business-case stage.

What was hard: social compliance audits. Their Turkish partner had a minor SMETA finding on working hours that took 4 months to close out to their own standard. Buyers who operate ESG-sensitive markets (Germany, Scandinavia, California) should expect SMETA / BSCI audits to matter more with Turkish partners, not less.

## Pattern 3: The one that didn't work — B2B electronics OEM

A €150M B2B electronics OEM tried to move mid-complexity PCBA assembly from Shenzhen to Hungary in 6 months. The business case looked good on paper: lead time reduction, no tariff risk, close to the OEM customer base.

What went wrong: they moved too fast (no pilot stage), committed 100% volume to one Hungarian supplier, and the supplier's ERP systems couldn't handle their variant complexity (~240 variants across 15 product lines). Month 4 saw delivery failures on 30% of POs.

Recovery: they paused, moved back to 70% China for critical SKUs, restarted with 30% Hungary on their 50 highest-volume variants only. 18 months later they're at 50/50.

**What this pattern teaches:** the playbook sequencing (pilot 15-25%, parallel 40-60%, ramp 60-80%) is not a suggestion. Skipping pilot stage is the most common failure mode we see, and it's why the 90-day plan on page 12 exists.
