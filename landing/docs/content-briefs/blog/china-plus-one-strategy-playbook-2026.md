## Copy angle (1 sentence)
China+1 is not nearshoring — it's a specific risk-mitigation posture where China stays your primary and you build a qualified backup — this playbook separates the strategy from the execution (category selection, supplier pilot, volume ramp, exit criteria).

## Must-include elements
- Strategy framing: China+1 ≠ China exit. Why most operators get this wrong. Quote-worthy: "China+1 is insurance, not divorce."
- The 5-criteria category prioritization: (1) supply shock sensitivity (high = first), (2) unit economics (commodity vs. engineered — commodities move easier), (3) certification portability, (4) IP sensitivity (IP-heavy = last or never), (5) customer contract flexibility.
- Country options by category archetype: Vietnam (electronics assembly, apparel), Mexico (US-market automotive, appliances), India (pharma APIs, textiles, IT services), Turkey (textiles, white goods), Eastern Europe (machining, automotive tier 2).
- The 90-day pilot template: Days 1-30 (source + qualify 10-15 alt suppliers, pick 2-3 for RFQ), Days 31-60 (sampling + quality validation), Days 61-90 (small pilot order + quality/lead-time measurement).
- Volume ramp model: how to move from 0% to 20% China+1 share over 12 months without disrupting China relationships. Who to tell, when.
- Exit criteria that make the +1 real, not theater: supplier qualified, tooling transferred or duplicated, production-grade samples approved, backup PO in place.
- Financial model: total cost of diversification (tooling, audits, higher unit cost year 1) vs. risk-adjusted cost of single-country exposure (probability × disruption cost).
